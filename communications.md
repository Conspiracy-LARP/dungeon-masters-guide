# How we communicate — cells, collaboration, and the strangers we'll never meet

*There is no forum, no membership and no boss. People organise in small cells, build on strangers they
will never identify, and are one another's audience. How to interweave your work with someone else's,
and why not knowing who made a thing is a feature rather than a gap.*

---

## 1. Interweaving etiquette

A short, human summary of how to be a good member of the troupe, because the whole thing runs on
goodwill rather than rules:

- **Offer freely.** Put your ideas up where others can find and use them. An offer nobody can see can't
  be built on.
- **Hold loosely.** Once you've offered, you don't own what happens next. Others will remix, extend,
  and cheerfully contradict you.
- **Communicate the offer.** When you build on someone, tell them. When you leave a hook for others,
  make it findable.
- **Celebrate the mutation.** If someone takes your idea somewhere strange, that's a win, not a theft.
  The prize of improv is being surprised by what your offer became.
- **Linking back is a handshake, not a claim.** Acknowledging another node honours it; it does not bind
  either of you to consistency.

If you happen to share a **back-channel** with other creators (a chat room where you compare notes and
drop offers), that's the fastest way to interweave: say what you're building and ask who wants a thread
pulled in. But there's no single room, no requirement to be in one, and no way to be sure who's out
there at all. How the community is (and isn't) organised is the whole of the next section.

---

## 2. How we organise: cells, not a command

There is no central forum, no membership roll, and no headquarters. By design, not by neglect. We
organise the way a loose insurgency does: in small **cells**. A cell is maybe five or so creators who
collaborate closely: trading offers, cross-linking each other's nodes, reading each other's drafts.
Some people belong to more than one cell at once, quietly carrying ideas between them. Others work
entirely alone and communicate *only through their actions* (through the work they publish) and never
meet a soul. There is no prescribed way to cooperate and there is **no boss**: nobody convenes the
group, assigns the work, or signs anything off. The structure is only ever whatever the creators make
it this week.

One consequence is worth sitting with: **you usually can't tell who made a thing.** Stumble across one
of our nodes in the wild and its author is invisible, unless they *chose* to leave a fingerprint (cast
themselves as the villain of the piece, say, or planted a clue only a certain person would think to
leave). That's a feature, not a gap. Part of the pleasure, for readers and creators alike, is *not
knowing for certain* who else is in on the joke. For all any of us know, some of the people we take for
sincere conspiracy theorists are running a LARP not so different from this one. You can't always tell
from the outside, and you don't need to.

So: you find work you love, you want to join in, and you can't work out who's behind it. Don't be
frustrated. That's the game working exactly as intended. Just **start making things.** Write some
fiction of your own; fold in the nodes you liked best (turn the camera back at them; see the engine in
`creator-kit.md`); and leave a few clues about how to reach *you*. In time the creators you admire may
feel the new thread pulling on theirs and reach out. Problem solved. Or they don't, and instead your
work draws an entirely new set of collaborators who found *you* first. Either way the world got bigger,
which is the only score that's kept.

One last thing, said plainly because the metaphor invites the wrong reading: **we are not an insurgency,
and we are certainly not terrorists.** We are fiction writers who happen to use a strange, diffuse,
identity-blurring *mode* of fiction. The cells and the anonymity are features of that mode, not a
conspiracy of our own. The only thing we are organised to do is make each other laugh and make the lie
look ridiculous. Wherever the raw material comes from, the instruction is always the same: **find what's
funny, build it into your world, and elevate your collaborators.**

---

## 3. We are the players too, and so are strangers we'll never meet

Here is a thing that is easy to miss: **the creators of this game are also its players.** We build these
interactive worlds partly for an audience who has no idea who we are or who is behind any of it. And we
are, at the very same time, each other's audience, discovering each other's nodes exactly the way a
stranger would. There is no clean line between making the game and playing it. You publish a thing; you
go wandering through the mesh; you find something that delights you; you build on it. You are an author
and a reader in the same afternoon.

Now push that one step further. Because the cells are small and don't overlap, **you will sometimes find
yourself playing alongside creators you have never met and may never identify** — people running a
similar game, who would sign this same manifesto in a heartbeat, but who arrived at it entirely on their
own. The friends you make along the way, so to speak. What do you do when you stumble onto brilliant work
that might be one of ours, or might be a total stranger's, or might not even *know* it is part of
anything at all? Exactly what you would do with anyone: **you yes-and it.** You fold their invention into
yours, extend your universe to touch theirs, and treat their idea as the gift it is, whether or not they
are aware of you, whether or not they ever find out. That way *both* layers grow at once: the fiction
(the shared world gets wider and stranger) and the meta-fiction (the loose, half-invisible community of
people playing this game gets wider too). Nobody had to be introduced for the world to expand.

And remember what the medium actually is. This is an **improvisation show made of text, HTML, and
interactive websites**, of fake intranets and fabricated filings and blogs and QR codes and every other
strange thing an LLM-enabled human with a big imagination can now conjure into being. That last part is
the quiet miracle of the moment we're in. It used to be that you might have a wonderful idea for a thing
to make and be stopped cold by lacking the skill to build it. Not any more. Now you only have to *think
of it*. The model builds the rest. Which means the one scarce ingredient, the only thing the
machine cannot supply, is the thing *you* bring: **finding what is funny about these horrible theories,
and expressing it inside the frame of the game we're all playing.** Bring that, and everything else is
just typing.
