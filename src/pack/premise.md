# The premise — what if it were all true

*The one idea the whole shared world rests on, and the only page describing what is true* inside *it.
Where the premise came from — Wilson and Shea, 1975 — and what follows from taking it seriously: a world
that must be staffed, old, self-contradicting, unbearably banal, and understood by nobody, not even the
people running it.*

---

## The question

**What if all the conspiracy theories were true?**

Not one of them. Not the plausible ones. **All of them, simultaneously.** The flat earth and the
kill-grid and the adrenochrome trade and the lizard people and the chemtrails and the faked moon landing
and the ones nobody has invented yet — all of it, at once, running right now, underneath everything.

That is the premise. Everything else in this pack is a consequence of taking it seriously.

## Where it comes from

The idea is not ours, and we don't pretend otherwise.

In the late 1960s, **Robert Anton Wilson** and **Robert Shea** were working the slush pile at *Playboy*,
reading letters from people who had discovered the truth about the Illuminati, the Bavarian bankers, the
Kennedy assassination, the fluoride. They could have laughed and binned them. Instead they asked the
question nobody asks: *what if the cranks are all right?* Not one of them — **all of them, including the
ones that flatly contradict each other.**

They wrote it across the late 1960s and early '70s; Dell published it in **1975**, as three paperbacks
in three consecutive months — *The Eye in the Pyramid* (September), *The Golden Apple* (October),
*Leviathan* (November).

The **_Illuminatus!_ Trilogy** is sprawling, chaotic and gloriously unreliable: every conspiracy is true
at once, the Discordians fight the Illuminati, Atlantis surfaces, and the reader is never told which
narrator to believe. It is very much a product of its moment, and it is still the funniest argument
anyone has made against taking conspiracy theory seriously: not *"you are wrong"*, but
*"fine — and here is what you have signed up for."*

**This project is a homage to that move, brought forward fifty years.** What we add is not the idea. It
is the medium:

- Wilson and Shea had a **novel** — one text, two authors, a narrator, covers, an ending. We have the
  **open internet**: no covers, no narrator, no ending, and no way to tell where it stops.
- They wrote about the conspiracies of *their* moment — Bavarian bankers, the JFK grassy knoll. We take
  the conspiracies of **ours** — 5G, ULEZ cameras, adrenochrome, the ones that arrived last Tuesday.
- They had to *describe* the Illuminati's paperwork. **We can just publish it.** A fake intranet, a
  leaked procurement ticket, a dead SOAP endpoint, a company that files real-looking accounts. The gag
  they could only narrate, we can leave lying on the actual internet for a stranger to find.

Wilson had the idea. We have the tooling he'd have killed for.

## And if that were true… what else would be true?

This is the useful half, and it's where the premise stops being a joke and starts being a *world*. Take
it seriously for ten seconds and things follow — necessarily, whether we like them or not.

**It would have to be staffed.** Someone runs the kill-grid. That someone has a job title, a line
manager, and a leaving do when they move to a competitor. The death-ray has a maintenance schedule,
because everything has a maintenance schedule. Nobody in history has ever run a global atrocity without
a rota dispute.

**It would have to be old.** Some of these theories are centuries old. So the organisations behind them
are centuries old — which means they predate the companies register, the telephone, and the concept of
the limited company. They have had time to become sclerotic, to acquire pension liabilities, and to
forget what half their own departments are for.

**It would have to contradict itself, constantly, and nobody would ever reconcile it.** The earth is
flat *and* the moon landing was faked — by different people, who may never have met. There is no summit
where the flat-earth cartel and the 5G operators align their stories, because there is no summit. Real
conspiracy theories contradict each other ceaselessly and it never slows them down; the mesh of
half-agreeing stories reads *more* true, not less. **You are not maintaining a bible. You are adding a
room to an impossible mansion that has no architect.**

**It would have to be banal.** This is the load-bearing one. An atrocity that must be *administered* —
budgeted, staffed, audited, insured, minuted — becomes facilities management. Not because we're being
clever, but because that is what happens to anything a large organisation does for long enough. An
atrocity run as facilities management cannot also be a terrifying secret. That's not a joke we impose on
the premise; it's a fact the premise cannot escape.

**And nobody would have the whole picture. Not even the conspirators.** This is the most Wilsonian
entailment and the most freeing one. In a world where every conspiracy is true, no single person can
hold it — the operatives are working from partial briefings and half-understood memos, exactly like
everyone else. There is no room where it all makes sense. **There is no room.**

## What follows for you

That last entailment is not decoration. It is the licence for how this whole project works:

- **Your cast is your own.** There is no canonical organisation, because in a world this size nobody
  could enumerate them. A node about ULEZ invents an outfit that runs directed-energy cameras; a node
  about flat earth invents border-wardens patrolling the ice wall. Entirely different companies,
  entirely different people, no shared org chart. You inherit nothing.
- **Contradicting another creator is not a bug.** It is the world behaving correctly. Two creators who
  share an organisation may describe it completely differently. That's what a real conspiracy landscape
  looks like from inside.
- **Nobody can be wrong about the lore**, because there is no lore — only rooms, and the mansion.
- **The paperwork is the point.** If the world is staffed, old, contradictory and banal, then the
  artifacts it sheds are memos and invoices and expired certificates. That's not a stylistic choice;
  it's what this world would actually leave lying around. (The craft of making those land is
  [`storytelling.md`](storytelling.md).)

## One thing this page is not

This is a description of the world **inside** the fiction. It is not a claim about the world outside it,
and the distinction matters enough to state plainly: none of this is true, we know none of it is true,
and the fact that it is *obviously* not true is the entire joke.

Conspiracy theories are not harmless, and we are not neutral about them — they hijack real problems and
hand people a lie instead of an answer. That argument, and what it costs, is
[`philosophy.md`](philosophy.md). The floor beneath the comedy is [`ethics.md`](ethics.md).

The premise is *"what if it were all true."* The answer this project keeps arriving at, artifact by
artifact, is: **then it would be unbelievably tedious, and somebody would still have to order the
staplers.**
