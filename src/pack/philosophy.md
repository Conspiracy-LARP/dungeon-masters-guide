# The philosophy — why we do this

*Conspiracy theory does not merely spread falsehood. It hijacks real questions and answers them with a
lie so lurid the real question can never be asked again. This is the argument for fighting that with
comedy rather than correction, and for why comedians and software developers turn out to be the right
people to try. The reasoning the rest of the pack states only in passing.*

---

## 1. The problem: conspiracy theory steals the conversation

Start with the harm, because the harm is the whole reason. The usual complaint about conspiracy theories
is that they are *false*, and they are. But falsehood is the least of it. Plenty of harmless things are
false. What makes conspiracy theory corrosive is something more specific and more damaging:

**It stops us having real conversations. It stops us solving real problems by handing us fake solutions
to fake versions of them.**

Every live conspiracy theory is parasitic on a genuine, serious concern:

- Surveillance and civil liberties are a real issue. Then "the ULEZ cameras are a genocide grid"
  arrives, and now you cannot raise a sober question about automated number-plate recognition without
  being sorted into the death-ray camp.
- Air quality and industrial pollution are real problems, until "chemtrails" swallow the subject
  whole and an honest argument about emissions or geoengineering becomes impossible.
- Child protection is a real, difficult, vital field, and then a trafficking-panic fantasy about
  celebrities and tunnels consumes all the oxygen, helps not one actual child, and teaches people to
  distrust the very institutions that do the protecting.
- Corporate power over public health, who profits from a war, who owns the infrastructure of a city,
  what we should do about taxation: every one of these is a legitimate thing to worry about, and every
  one has a lurid, unfalsifiable lie squatting in its place.

That is the injury. The conspiracy theory doesn't just add a falsehood to the world; it **evicts the
real question and occupies the ground it stood on.** Once the mast is a death-ray, telecoms policy is
un-discussable. The fake solution (*name the cabal, share the meme, "do your own research"*) feels like
engagement while guaranteeing that the real problem is never touched. The grifter gets an audience; the
real problem gets abandoned. That theft is what we are against.

## 2. Our response: we are comedians and software developers, so we fight with what we have

So what do we do about it? We could write another earnest debunking essay. The internet has millions of
those, and they change almost no one's mind, because you cannot argue someone out of a position that
feels like identity and community and the thrill of secret knowledge.

We are not, mostly, epidemiologists or telecoms engineers or policy experts. We are **comedians and
creative software developers.** And you fight with the weapons you actually have. Ours are two:
**technical ingenuity** and **humour.** We can build convincing things on the web quickly and cheaply,
and we can find the joke inside a grim subject. So we solve this problem the way we like to solve every
problem: by building something clever and funny, and letting it do the work an argument never could.

The something we build is a satire that operates by *agreement* rather than contradiction. We do not tell
the conspiracy theorist they are wrong. We say: **"Suppose you're right. Suppose it's all exactly as you
say."** And then we build the world that would have to exist if it were true, and we render its
world-ending horror as what it would *actually* be: tedious, banal, corporate business-as-usual. A
kill-grid with a maintenance backlog. An atrocity with an SLA. A death-ray whose real problem is a broken
SharePoint web part and an argument about the kettle. The incongruity is the joke, and the joke is the
debunk, because an atrocity run as facilities management cannot also be a terrifying secret. We don't
refute the theory; we agree with it so completely and so literally that it collapses under its own weight.

## 3. The method: diegetic storytelling, indistinguishable on purpose

We deliver this the way conspiracy theory itself is delivered. The *form* is half the argument.
Conspiracy theory is never a single labelled story; it is a diffuse mesh of artifacts scattered across
the culture, which the believer assembles for themselves and therefore cannot be talked out of. So we
adopt exactly that mode. We build **diegetic** fiction: fake intranets, fabricated filings, straight-faced
corporate websites, leaked memos, blogs in a character's voice. A mesh of mundane, real-looking things,
with no author's voice and no "this is satire" label, that a reader assembles into a picture for
themselves. (The `creator-kit.md` is the working manual for building all of this.)

And we make it, on purpose, **indistinguishable at a glance from the real thing.** This is the audacious
part, so it must be stated plainly: the goal is to build satire that a committed conspiracy practitioner
cannot tell apart from genuine conspiracy theory. If we can do that, we will have proved the thing we set
out to prove: that these beliefs are so untethered from reason that **conspiracy theory and a joke about
conspiracy theory are indistinguishable**, even to the believer. The theorist who nods along to our
invented kill-grid delivers, in that nod, the verdict on their own method: there is no test they can
apply, no way to tell our fabrication from their "truth." *That* is the debunk: not that we said the
theory was absurd, but that we demonstrated no one, including its own adherents, can distinguish it from
something we made up on a Tuesday.

## 4. The bugs: why indistinguishable is safe, and never fuel

"Indistinguishable from real conspiracy theory" would be reckless (it would just be *making more
conspiracy theory*) were it not for the one thing that makes it satire instead: **the tells are always
already inside.** Every artifact is salted with deliberate absurdities. We call them the **bugs**, or the
Easter eggs (`storytelling.md` is the full craft of them). A board of directors that is secretly the
cast of *Dad's Army*. A company incorporated in the year 1600, older than the register that lists it. A
QR code that decodes to a limerick. These are not mistakes; they are jokes wearing the costume of
mistakes, planted and primed and waiting.

They do triple duty. They are funny to the reader who is in on it. They are a landmine under anyone who
tries to weaponise the work as "evidence." And because they are absurd on their face, they are the
standing proof that the whole thing was always a performance. The work can pass for real *because* the
proof that it never was is baked into it. Camouflage on the outside; a joke all the way down.

## 5. The reserved right to detonate

Which leads to the last principle, and the most satisfying one. Because the bugs are always in there,
we are never lying. We are only *waiting*. And we **reserve the right to expose the joke, to detonate
our Easter eggs, at the moment of our choosing**, which is, naturally, the moment least convenient to the
conspiracy influencers who have been treating our fiction, or fiction like it, as gospel. The satire lies
dormant in perfect camouflage, woven quietly into the cultural landscape; and then, when it is most
useful and most funny to do so, the camouflage falls away and the whole edifice stands revealed for
exactly what it always was: a joke, and, by that same stroke, a mirror held up to the "real" theories it
was indistinguishable from.

Indistinguishable going in; unmistakable coming out. That gap is the payload, and it is why we can wear
perfect camouflage and still, always, be telling the truth. We are not here to add to the noise. We are
here to demonstrate, cheaply and cleverly and with a straight face, that the emperor of conspiracy has no
clothes, and to keep our hand on the curtain.

## 6. It's a game, and it's a fight worth joining

Two things about this project are true at once, and both matter.

**First: it's a game, and it is meant to be fun and harmless.** Nobody should get hurt. In particular,
this is **never an attack on a person.** We do not target individuals, we do not expose or harass a human
being, and we never make a real person the butt of the joke. It is play: elaborate, technical,
straight-faced play. And if it ever stops being fun and harmless, something has gone wrong.

**Second: it is, all the same, a fight, and against *ideas* we are unrelenting.** We attack harmful
ideas without mercy and without apology. That distinction, savage to the idea and gentle to the person, is
the whole ethic, and it is not a loophole. Some people will insist that an attack on an idea they hold
*is* an attack on them: that to mock the theory is to mock the believer. **We have no sympathy for that
position.** Ideas are not people; a belief is not a body; and the demand that criticism of a claim be
treated as a personal wound is precisely the move by which bad ideas make themselves un-criticisable. We
decline it, politely and completely.

Where our sympathy *does* go is to the people conspiracy theory actually harms. The communities whose
infrastructure is vandalised because someone decided a mast or a camera was a weapon. The neighbours who
pick up the public bill (in higher costs and higher taxes) for repairing what conspiracy-fuelled
antisocial behaviour destroys. The families pulled apart, the vulnerable people fleeced, the honest public
servants harassed for doing their jobs. Those are the injured parties, and they are who this is *for*.

**A word on politics, because the metaphors invite the question.** We are allied with no political party,
and we take no side in the ordinary left–right quarrel — creators here hold every conventional politics
there is. But it would be dishonest to pretend the work is neutral. This is, by necessity, a **political
operation** in the oldest and broadest sense: we stand with the **Enlightenment**. With reason,
evidence, and the shared civilisational agreement to settle disagreements by argument rather than by
fantasy. That is not a left cause or a right cause. It is the ground both of them stand on. And it is
exactly the ground conspiracy theory attacks.

Be clear about what that does and does not mean, because it's easily mistaken. The author of any given
node may hold strong opinions about the actual policy questions: whether environmental taxes are wise,
whether enforcement cameras belong on our streets at all. Those are real, arguable questions, with a
serious case on **both** sides and a conversation worth having. **This project takes no view on
them, and that is the entire point.** Because the moment a sizeable part of the population insists that the
environmental-monitoring camera is *really a deadly energy weapon*, that conversation is not won. It is
simply **over.** You cannot debate the merits of a policy with someone who believes the hardware is a
murder device. The democratic process seizes up; the streets fill instead with vandals and vigilantes
tearing the cameras down. And whichever side of the real argument you were on, that is a defeat for
everyone, because the argument we actually needed to have has been lifted off the table entirely.

So the invitation is deliberately open here. **Whichever side you land on, for the policy or against it,
we invite you to play this game if what you care about is reclaiming the forum for healthy, constructive
debate.** You do not have to agree with your fellow players about the cameras, or the taxes, or anything
else. You only have to agree that the conversation about them should remain *possible.* That is the one
commitment this project asks for, and it is one a reasonable person of any politics can make.

There is even a lever to hand. The conspiracy theorists are already among us, whether they know it or not,
dismantling that conversation, and the recommendation algorithms that boosted their nonsense did it
blindly: they optimise for engagement and care nothing for the democratic damage of amplifying a lie. But
the **same algorithm is available to us.** The very virality that carried the conspiracy can carry its
undoing. That is precisely why we reach for **diegetic storytelling**: a satire that travels the channels
the conspiracy travels, that is *discovered* the way the conspiracy is discovered, and that examines,
from the inside and in the conspiracy's own idiom, exactly how these ideas are impinging on our culture. We
don't shout at the algorithm. We feed it a better joke.

Because here, finally, is why conspiracy theory is dangerous, more dangerous than any single
falsehood it happens to peddle. **It eliminates the possibility of healthy discussion in the first
place.** A theory that is unfalsifiable, that reads every objection as proof of the plot and every
objector as a paid agent, doesn't merely win an argument. It *ends the practice of arguing.* It pulls up
the floor that reasonable disagreement stands on. So we invite everyone, of whatever politics, to come
together inside this one strange creative project and defend the thing we should all hold dear: the
simple, precious, endangered possibility of a reasonable conversation. That is the fight. It happens to be
a very funny one.
