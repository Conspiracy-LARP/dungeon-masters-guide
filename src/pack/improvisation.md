# What improvisation teaches us about world-building

*Companion to `creator-kit.md`. This whole project is a piece of improvised theatre — just a very slow
one, performed not on a stage over an evening but across the open web over months, in text, hypertext,
and interactive applications. Everything good about how improv troupes build a world together transfers
directly to how we build ours. These are the principles. None is a rule with an enforcer; they are simply
what makes the collaboration work.*

---

## 1. "Yes, and" — accept, then add

The foundation of all improv, and of this. You **accept the offer on the table** — the conspiracy's
premise, or another creator's contribution — as real, and then you **add** to it. Acknowledge the reality
someone has put in front of you, and build the next thing on top of it. That's the whole motor. A scene
(or a world) dies the instant someone says "no, that's not how it is." So the first discipline is never to
negate — always to accept, and extend.

But "accept" is subtler than "agree." Simple agreement — *yes, that's true, and here's more of it* — is
only the most obvious form of acknowledgment. The richer form is to **accept the base reality and then
react to it from a completely different point of view.** You don't have to *like* the thing you're
acknowledging, or endorse it, or even have your character believe it is good; you only have to grant that
it *exists* in the world, and then let a character with their own lens respond to it honestly. The
horrified blogger, the proud perpetrator, and the bored functionary can all acknowledge the very same base
reality — the organisation is real, the grid is running — and react in three utterly opposed ways. All
three are "yes, and"; not one of them is a negation. **Acknowledging a reality is not the same as agreeing
with it**, and that gap — between the shared fact and the clashing points of view upon it — is where much
of the best material lives. (It is also how many separate nodes can share one world honestly; see the
divergent-viewpoints note in `storytelling.md`.)

## 2. Radical acceptance — don't fight the worlds other people build

This follows straight from "yes, and," and it's worth stating strongly, because it's where the instinct to
control will fight you.

**Do not fight the worlds other players build.** When someone takes their content in a creative direction
— one that doesn't fit yours, or that you wouldn't have chosen — your reflex may be to retcon it, correct
it, or declare them wrong. Resist that completely. Instead:

- **First, try to harmonise.** Find a *creative* way to reconcile their world with yours. If their
  timeline contradicts yours, maybe there were two front companies; maybe a record was altered; maybe both
  are true and the discrepancy is *itself* a clue. Harmonising costs you nothing and adds diegetic
  weirdness — and in a show-don't-tell world, a denser, stranger, self-explaining mesh reads as *more*
  real, not less.
- **If you honestly can't harmonise — or you find another player's ideas genuinely offensive — just
  ignore it.** Don't fight it, don't publish a rebuttal, don't try to overwrite it. Simply turn your
  attention to another corner of the shared world and build there. Non-engagement is a complete and
  honourable response. Nobody is obliged to weave with anybody.

And crucially: **not everything has to make sense.** We do not expect the shared world to be consistent,
any more than the real world is. There will be contradictions, loose ends, and confusions — and that is
not a bug. From a *reader's* point of view, they can never be sure whether an inconsistency is a mistake
or a sophisticated stroke: a complex narrative told through an unreliable narrator, a deliberate seam left
for someone to pull. That ambiguity is a gift. It makes the whole mesh feel like a real, contested,
half-hidden history rather than a tidy authored fiction. Let the contradictions stand.

## 3. Don't worry — follow your muse, and trust the troupe

The last principle is the most freeing: **don't worry. Go and create.** Follow your muse, make the thing
that delights you, and trust that the other players have your back. Improv only works when everyone
extends everyone else that trust.

Del Close put the whole ethic in one line: **treat your fellow players like poets and geniuses.** In
practice that cuts two ways:

- When you make something, trust that others will receive it generously.
- When you look at *their* work, give it the same generosity — **critique it as though it were a great
  work of literature.** Assume there is intent and craft in it, even where you don't yet see it, and go
  looking for what's brilliant. Extract every ounce of fun and invention you can from it — and then feed
  that fun back into your own system, and build.

That is the engine of a growing world: not everyone policing everyone else for consistency, but everyone
mining everyone else for delight.

## 4. Turning the camera

Here is the technique that does the actual building — and the one that most distinguishes *this* medium
from live improv. On a stage, the whole scene is in front of the audience at once; the performance is the
thing happening now, in one place. Our performance is slower and stranger: it unfolds across text,
hypertext, links, and interactive web applications, over a long time, with no single stage. So the
fundamental directorial act here is not *performing* — it is **deciding where to point the camera.** Which
corner of the world to film next; which page to build; which thread to link. Turning the camera is how a
world with no stage grows.

There are three ways to turn it.

### Turn it OUT — to generate

Take the premise and film the part nobody thinks to film. If the ULEZ cameras are a control grid,
*someone runs them* — so film the operators' break room. Pick from this menu whenever you need the next
thing to build:

- **Who runs it?** The org, the department, the two-person satellite office.
- **Who profits?** The contractor, the shell company, the procurement chain.
- **Who covers it up?** The PR firm, the "debunker," the disclosure-control desk.
- **The operatives.** The individual humans with lanyards, rotas, and grievances.
- **The boring version.** Whatever the horror is, film it as facilities management — the SLA, the ticket,
  the expired certificate, the memo about the kettle.
- **Who got hurt?** By *inference only* — the claim form, the compensation schedule, never the harm
  itself (see `ethics.md`).
- **The adjacent theory.** If your corner is real, the *neighbouring* delusion is probably real too.

### Turn it TOWARD ANOTHER CREATOR — to offer

Point the camera at someone else's node and build a link from your world into theirs. If another player
built a company that suppresses the flat-earth truth, and you built a company that runs the ULEZ grid —
two *entirely separate* casts — then maybe they share a **registrar**; maybe the same PR mercenary
consults for both; maybe you invent a **partnership**, a newly-minted parent both worlds turn out to
answer to. The link is something you *create* to join two independent worlds; it isn't a canon you were
both always part of. Menu:

- **A shared supplier or registrar** (the same little firm incorporated both front companies).
- **A shared contract or client.**
- **A shared person** (one operative moonlights across both worlds).
- **A handoff** (your organisation subcontracts a job to theirs).
- **A rivalry** (two rival organisations, or two feuding departments of a parent you invent).
- **A leaked document** that mentions both.

When you do this, tell them — it's a courtesy and it's how the mesh knits (see `communications.md`). But
then **let go.** They are **not obliged to reciprocate.** They might take your offer and run somewhere you
never imagined; they might ignore it; they might contradict it. That's not a betrayal — it's exactly how
improv works. Be proud you inspired something new, especially when it mutates beyond recognition.

### Turn it TOWARD YOURSELF — to be inspired

The reverse motion, and just as important. Sometimes you'll *observe* something great that another player
has made, and you use it to turn the camera back onto **your own** world — pulling their brilliant idea
in as inspiration and building it into what you're making. This is "yes, and" pointed at yourself: you
saw a gift, you accepted it, and you add to your own world because of it. Celebrate the creator who
sparked it, whether or not they ever know — being inspired is a form of applause.

### The shape it all builds toward

Do all this for long enough, in enough hands, and the result is an **early-web hypertext maze**: a mass of
separately-owned pages that cross-link into something no single author could have planned, where following
the threads is the whole pleasure. Nobody drew the map. That is precisely the point.

## 5. Play to the top of your intelligence

One more principle, borrowed straight from the Upright Citizens Brigade, and it is the quality bar for the
whole shared world: **play to the top of your intelligence.** Don't dumb it down. Don't create stupid
characters and foolish companies. When a person feels pressure to be funny, the lazy reflex is to make a
character *arbitrarily* stupid — to reach for the cheap laugh of idiocy. Resist it completely. UCB's whole
teaching here is that comedy comes from an honest, grounded, *intelligent* reaction to an absurd premise,
not from manufactured stupidity — you commit to the character as real as you possibly can, and you treat
your audience as at least as clever as you are.

There's a hard-headed, in-world reason this matters especially for *us*. In the real world, dummies don't
have impact. A person who acts foolishly doesn't run a secret programme; a corporation that makes stupid
decisions goes out of business very quickly. So a stupid character or a foolish company simply *isn't
believable* as part of a vast, functioning, sinister machine — it breaks the reality we depend on. And
note the deeper point, the one that connects to character-as-point-of-view: **stupidity is not a point of
view.** It's not a lens on the world at all — it's merely a propensity to act illogically, against the
character's or the company's own best interest. It gives you nothing to write from. A *smart* villain
pursuing a monstrous goal with cold competence is frightening, funny, and real; a stupid one is just
noise.

So for the good of the shared world: make your people clever, your companies competent, and your evil
*efficient*. Let the horror come from how well the machine works, not how badly. Play everyone — hero,
villain, functionary — at the very top of their intelligence, and the whole edifice holds together.
